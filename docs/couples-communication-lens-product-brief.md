# Couples Communication Lens — Product Brief

## Root Problem

Two people can be talking about the same thing while using completely different words because they are attending to different parts of the same reality.

Example:

- Person A sees: **a horse**
- Person B sees: **landscape, trees, a horse in the country**

They are not necessarily disagreeing.

They may be using different perceptual and communication lenses:

- object/category lens
- scene/context lens
- detail lens
- spatial/layout lens
- narrative lens
- emotional/atmosphere lens
- verbal/label lens

The goal is not to label one person as right or wrong.

The goal is to help couples understand:

1. what each person notices first
2. what each person compresses into words
3. what context each person assumes is obvious
4. where meaning gets lost between perception and communication
5. how to translate between their lenses

## Product Goal

Build a lightweight test for couples that helps them understand how each person sees, encodes, and communicates the world.

The output should help couples say:

> “Oh, you are not ignoring what I mean. You are entering the same scene through a different door.”

## What This Is Not

This is not a clinical assessment.

This is not a personality test that says “you are visual” or “you are verbal.”

This is not a compatibility score.

This is not a tool for proving one partner communicates better.

## What This Is

A relational communication mirror.

It should reveal patterns like:

- One partner names the object; the other describes the whole scene.
- One partner communicates in labels; the other communicates in context.
- One partner starts with structure; the other starts with story.
- One partner remembers the visual snapshot; the other remembers the meaning or sequence.
- One partner thinks the point is obvious; the other needs the surrounding frame.

## Core Research Questions

1. When someone says they are a visual person, what do they actually mean?
2. Does how someone sees match how they communicate?
3. Does “visual” mean objects, scenes, spatial layout, imagery vividness, or something else?
4. When two people describe the same thing differently, which lens changed?
5. Can we help couples translate between lenses before conflict/frustration starts?

## Test Design Direction

The test should be paired, not only individual.

Each partner should respond to the same prompts independently, then compare.

Prompt types:

1. **Same image, different first grab**
   - What did you notice first?
   - What did you think mattered?
   - What would you say to describe it?

2. **Same memory, different encoding**
   - Do you remember the scene, the sequence, the words, the feeling, or the context?

3. **Same explanation task, different output**
   - Would you explain with a label, a story, a diagram, a list, or a scene?

4. **Same disagreement, different assumed context**
   - What did you assume the other person already understood?

## Important Lens Categories

Use separate constructs instead of one “visual vs verbal” axis:

- Object/category: “a horse”
- Scene/context: “a horse in the country with trees”
- Detail/features: color, shape, texture, motion
- Spatial/layout: where things are in relation
- Gist/atmosphere: vibe, mood, overall scene
- Narrative/sequence: what happened, what comes next
- Verbal/semantic anchor: labels, names, short handles
- Communication output: how they explain it to someone else

## Output Concept

Couples should receive a comparison report like:

- Partner A tends to compress scenes into object labels.
- Partner B tends to preserve surrounding context.
- Partner A may think they are being concise.
- Partner B may feel important context is being omitted.
- Translation move: Partner A should add one sentence of context; Partner B should name the central object/decision earlier.

## North Star

Help couples understand the sentence beneath the disagreement:

> “We are looking at the same thing, but our brains are choosing different handles for it.”
